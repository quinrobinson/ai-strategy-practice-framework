# AI Practice Maturity Model

Assess your team's sustained capability to run AI initiatives well — across six dimensions, from initial to leading.

The readiness audit evaluates whether a team is ready for a specific initiative. The maturity model evaluates whether a team is building the sustained capability to run AI initiatives well over time. These are different questions. A team can pass a readiness audit and still have no practice — they got lucky on one project without developing the systems that make success repeatable.

## Goal

**Decision enabled:** Where the team's AI practice sits today — and where to invest to move it forward.
**Output:** An AI Practice Maturity Assessment — scored across six dimensions with a current level, a target level, and a prioritized improvement roadmap.
**What this unlocks:** A strategic foundation for the AI Practice Lead. Turns "we need to get better at AI" from a vague aspiration into a structured, prioritized improvement plan. Prevents the pattern where teams repeat the same failures across projects because the practice never improved between them.

## When to Use

- Annually — as a standing team-level assessment
- When a new AI Practice Lead or head of AI joins
- When multiple AI initiatives have failed and the team wants to diagnose the root cause
- Before making a significant investment in AI capability (tools, training, headcount)
- At the close of a project cycle — use the retrospective to update the maturity model

## The Six Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Strategy & Direction

Does the team have a clear, shared understanding of why they use AI and what they're trying to achieve?

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **1 — Initial** | AI use is ad hoc. No shared strategy. Individual projects driven by tool availability or curiosity. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some awareness of AI's potential. Informal discussions about strategy. No documented direction. |
| **3 — Defined** | A documented AI strategy exists. It's known to the team. It's reviewed occasionally. |
| **4 — Managed** | AI strategy is connected to business outcomes. Leadership reviews progress against it. It evolves based on results. |
| **5 — Leading** | AI strategy is a competitive differentiator. It informs product roadmap, hiring, and investment decisions. The team can articulate their AI direction to any stakeholder in under two minutes. |

### Dimension 2: Problem Definition

Does the team consistently start with problems, not tools?

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **1 — Initial** | Tools are selected before problems are defined. Solution-first thinking is the default. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some projects start with problem statements, but the practice is inconsistent. Tools still drive many decisions. |
| **3 — Defined** | Problem qualification is a recognized step. Most projects attempt it, with varying rigor. |
| **4 — Managed** | Problem qualification is required before any AI resource commitment. The team has a standard format and uses it consistently. |
| **5 — Leading** | Problem qualification is embedded in the team's culture. Team members challenge each other on problem clarity. "What problem are we solving?" is asked reflexively. |

### Dimension 3: Data Capability

Does the team have the data infrastructure, governance, and skills to power AI initiatives reliably?

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **1 — Initial** | Data is siloed, poorly documented, and difficult to access. Data quality is unknown. No data governance. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some data is accessible and documented. Basic governance exists but is inconsistently applied. AI projects frequently discover data gaps mid-build. |
| **3 — Defined** | Data inventory exists. Data governance is documented and followed for major data sources. Data quality is assessed before AI projects begin. |
| **4 — Managed** | Data infrastructure supports AI development. Data lineage is tracked. Consent and legal basis are documented. Data quality is monitored continuously. |
| **5 — Leading** | Data is a strategic asset. Data strategy informs AI strategy. Privacy by design is standard. The team can answer any data question about a live AI system within hours. |

### Dimension 4: Risk & Ethics

Does the team proactively identify and manage AI-specific risks, including fairness and responsibility?

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **1 — Initial** | Risks are not considered until something goes wrong. No responsible AI practice. Bias is not evaluated. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some awareness of AI risks. Ad hoc risk conversations happen but no structured process. Ethics concerns are raised informally. |
| **3 — Defined** | Risk mapping and responsible AI assessment are recognized steps. They happen on most projects, with varying quality. |
| **4 — Managed** | Risk mapping, responsible AI assessment, and fairness evaluation are required before deployment. Results are reviewed by a named owner. Incident response is documented. |
| **5 — Leading** | Risk and ethics are integrated into every AI decision, not treated as separate steps. The team has documented its ethical position on AI and applies it consistently. External stakeholders trust the team's responsible AI practice. |

### Dimension 5: Measurement & Learning

Does the team measure AI outcomes and systematically learn from each initiative?

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **1 — Initial** | AI initiatives are not measured against defined outcomes. No retrospectives. The team doesn't know if their AI work is succeeding. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some projects define metrics. Retrospectives happen occasionally. Learning is informal and not shared across projects. |
| **3 — Defined** | Outcome definition is standard. Retrospectives happen at project close. Lessons are documented but not systematically applied. |
| **4 — Managed** | Every AI initiative has defined outcomes, baselines, and guardrails. Retrospectives produce feed-forward inputs to the next project. The failure mode library is actively updated. |
| **5 — Leading** | The team's measurement practice compounds — each project makes the next one better. AI outcomes are reviewed at the leadership level. The team can demonstrate the ROI of their AI practice across multiple initiatives. |

### Dimension 6: Culture & Capability

Does the team have the skills, mindset, and organizational support to do AI work well?

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **1 — Initial** | Limited AI literacy. No dedicated AI roles or skills. Leadership is unaware or skeptical. No investment in capability building. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some individuals have AI skills. Basic AI literacy is growing. Leadership is interested but not actively supportive. Occasional training happens. |
| **3 — Defined** | AI literacy is sufficient for the team to make informed AI decisions. Some dedicated AI roles exist. Leadership supports AI investment. Training is planned. |
| **4 — Managed** | The team has the skills to run the full ASPF lifecycle without significant external support. Leadership is an active AI champion. Capability building is ongoing and structured. |
| **5 — Leading** | The team is recognized internally and externally for AI practice excellence. They contribute to the broader organization's AI capability. They attract talent because of their AI culture. |
