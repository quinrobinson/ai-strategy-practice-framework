# Stakeholder Interviews

Understand what each person actually cares about — before they're in a room together.

Group workshops surface positions. Individual conversations surface truth. In a workshop, Derek will say what he thinks he should say in front of his CFO and VP of Sales. In a 30-minute one-on-one, he'll tell you what he actually believes. That difference is the engagement.

This skill operates in three modes depending on what access is available. The output — stakeholder profiles, fault line map, workshop positioning — is the same in all three. The confidence level attached to each insight is what changes.

## Goal

**Decision enabled:** How to position and facilitate the workshop given what each stakeholder privately believes, fears, and needs.
**Output:** A Stakeholder Intelligence Brief — individual profiles with goals, concerns, and private diagnosis; a fault line map showing where stakeholders agree and disagree; workshop positioning guidance for sequencing, framing, and what to avoid.
**What this unlocks:** A workshop the practitioner walks into knowing the room — not discovering it in real time. The difference between a practitioner who surprises the client and one who gets surprised.

## When to Use

- After Act 1 (pre-meeting prep) and before Act 2 (workshop)
- When stakeholder interviews have been completed and need to be synthesized
- When interviews aren't possible and the practitioner needs informed assumptions
- When partial interview access exists and gaps need to be filled

---

## Operating Modes

### Mode A — Full Interviews
Individual 30-minute calls with each key stakeholder completed before the workshop. Practitioner feeds interview notes to ASPF. Full synthesis produced.

### Mode B — Partial Access
Some stakeholders interviewed, others not. ASPF synthesizes confirmed insights from interviews and generates role-based assumptions for gaps. Assumptions clearly flagged.

### Mode C — No Access
No pre-workshop interviews possible. ASPF generates a complete role-based stakeholder hypothesis map from the engagement brief and stakeholder list alone. Everything labeled as assumed — calibrated during the first 20 minutes of the workshop.

## The 30-Minute Interview Guide

Use this structure for each individual stakeholder call. The questions are sequenced deliberately — build trust before probing for tension.

### Opening (2 minutes)
Set the frame clearly at the start:

> "I have about 30 minutes and I want to use it entirely on your perspective — not pitching, not presenting. I'm trying to understand the business from where you sit before we get everyone in a room together. Everything you share here informs how we approach the workshop, and nothing gets attributed to you specifically."

The confidentiality signal matters. People say different things when they know it won't be repeated verbatim.

---

### Block 1 — Their View of the Business Problem (8 minutes)

These questions surface their private diagnosis — which is almost always different from the official organizational position.

1. "From where you sit, what's the core problem the business needs to solve right now — in your own words, not the company line?"

2. "What do you think is causing it — and what do most people get wrong about the diagnosis?"

3. "If you could change one thing about how this organization operates that would have the biggest impact, what would it be?"

*What you're listening for:* The gap between their answer and what the CEO said the problem is. That gap is a fault line. Write it down.

---

### Block 2 — Their Experience with Technology and Change (8 minutes)

These questions surface their risk posture, prior trauma, and what they'll defend.

4. "What's your honest read on how technology initiatives have gone here in the past — what worked, what didn't, and why?"

5. "What would have to be true about this engagement for you to feel like it was worth your time and the company's investment?"

6. "What's the thing you'd most want us to avoid doing — based on what you've seen go wrong before?"

*What you're listening for:* Specific failures they reference. The more specific, the more it's still raw. That's where their resistance will come from in the workshop.

---

### Block 3 — Their Stake in the Outcome (8 minutes)

These questions surface what success and failure mean for them personally — not just organizationally.

7. "If this engagement goes well, what changes for you and your team?"

8. "If it goes sideways, what's the risk — to the business and to you personally?"

9. "Who else in the organization has a strong point of view on this that we should make sure we understand?"

*What you're listening for:* Personal exposure. People who have something to lose are either allies (if protected) or blockers (if threatened). Know which before the workshop.

---

### Close (4 minutes)

10. "Is there anything you think we need to know going into this that I haven't asked about — or that won't come up naturally in a group setting?"

11. "What would make you feel like the workshop was a success?"

*These two questions often produce the most valuable information in the entire call.* The first surfaces what the person has been waiting to say. The second gives you their personal definition of win — which may be completely different from the organizational definition.

## Role-Based Assumption Library

When interviews aren't possible, use these role archetypes to generate informed assumptions. Every assumption must be labeled as such — never present assumed insight as confirmed.

### CEO / President
**Typical primary concern:** Competitive position and business survival. They are thinking about the board, the market, and their legacy.
**Typical fear:** Committing resources to something that doesn't move the needle — or that makes them look like they chased a trend.
**Typical private diagnosis:** Usually more pessimistic about the organization's capabilities than they say publicly. Often knows what the real problem is but hasn't said it out loud yet.
**What earns trust:** Demonstrating you understand their specific business — not AI in general. Speaking in P&L terms, not technology terms.
**What kills trust:** Leading with capability before understanding the problem. Using transformation language before you've earned the right.

### CFO / Finance Lead
**Typical primary concern:** Capital allocation risk and the cost of being wrong. They've seen initiatives fail and they carry the financial consequence.
**Typical fear:** A third failed implementation. Being the person who approved the spend.
**Typical private diagnosis:** More skeptical than they show. Often privately believes the problem is operational discipline, not technology.
**What earns trust:** Staged investment with clear decision gates. Showing you understand TCO, not just build cost. Demonstrating reversibility.
**What kills trust:** A large upfront number without a clear off-ramp. Vague ROI claims. Any language that sounds like "we'll figure out the value as we go."

### VP of Sales / Chief Revenue Officer
**Typical primary concern:** Making number. Everything else is secondary.
**Typical fear:** Technology that slows down the sales motion or distracts the team from selling.
**Typical private diagnosis:** Usually believes the problem is something they can directly control — pricing, relationships, territory, product. Will resist diagnoses that implicate the sales process.
**What earns trust:** Showing you understand what a rep's day actually looks like. Framing any AI work as making reps better, not replacing their judgment.
**What kills trust:** Anything that sounds like it will add process or reporting burden to the sales team. Abstract strategy that doesn't connect to quota.

### CTO / IT Director / Head of Engineering
**Typical primary concern:** System stability and not being blamed for something that breaks.
**Typical fear:** Scope creep that touches production systems. Vendors who overpromise technical integration.
**Typical private diagnosis:** Usually has the clearest view of the real technical constraints — and is the most likely to know what can't be done. Often right about things leadership doesn't want to hear.
**What earns trust:** Demonstrating you understand the actual technical environment — not a hypothetical greenfield. Asking about what's fragile before proposing what's possible.
**What kills trust:** Glossing over integration complexity. Proposing cloud migration without acknowledging the migration risk. Showing a reference architecture that doesn't account for their specific constraints.

### COO / Head of Operations
**Typical primary concern:** Process reliability. Operations leaders are paid to keep things running — disruption is their enemy.
**Typical fear:** An AI initiative that creates new operational dependencies without a clear support model.
**Typical private diagnosis:** Usually focuses on the human side — who will actually run this, what training is needed, what happens when it breaks.
**What earns trust:** Showing you've thought about the operational model, not just the technology. Having a clear answer to "who runs this in 18 months."
**What kills trust:** Building something elegant that nobody can operate. Handoffs without support plans.

### PE Observer / Board Member
**Typical primary concern:** Portfolio narrative and exit optionality. They are thinking about how this looks to the next buyer.
**Typical fear:** A portfolio company that's visibly behind on AI in a market where AI-readiness is being priced into valuations.
**Typical private diagnosis:** Often more sophisticated about AI than they let on — they're seeing it across many portfolio companies. Their concern is whether the company can credibly execute, not whether AI is real.
**What earns trust:** A credible, sequenced plan — not a vision deck. Evidence that the team understands the specific business model, not generic AI transformation.
**What kills trust:** Buzzword-heavy decks with no operational specificity. Any sense that the engagement is about making AI sound exciting rather than solving the business problem.

### HR / People Lead
**Typical primary concern:** Workforce impact and change management. They are thinking about the people who will be affected.
**Typical fear:** An announcement of AI that creates fear and attrition before a plan is in place.
**Typical private diagnosis:** Usually knows where the resistance will come from before anyone else does. Has informal intelligence about how employees feel.
**What earns trust:** Showing that workforce transition is part of the plan from day one, not an afterthought. Asking what they've already heard from employees about AI.
**What kills trust:** A technology roadmap with no people plan. Language about "eliminating" roles before a redeployment or upskilling plan exists.

## Applying This to Meridian Supply Partners

*Example application of Mode B — three stakeholders interviewed, two assumed.*

If you could only get three pre-workshop calls at Meridian, prioritize in this order:

**1. Derek Vallance (CEO) — required**
His private diagnosis is the whole engagement. If he tells you privately what he actually believes is causing the wallet share erosion, you can position the workshop around solving his real problem rather than the stated one.

**2. Diane Luo (IT Director) — required**
She knows exactly what the Epicor environment can and cannot support. She also knows what previous implementations actually broke and why. That information changes every recommendation you make.

**3. Carla Rhem (CFO) — strongly recommended**
Understanding specifically which two implementations failed — and what they cost — gives you the framing you need to make the financial case for this engagement without triggering her pattern-matching for "this is going to go wrong again."

**Marcus Fitch (VP Sales) — assume from role**
His archetype is well-established. High-influence, competing diagnosis, protects his team. His interview would be valuable but his behavior is predictable enough to manage without it.

**Rene Castillo (PE Observer) — assume from role, brief interaction**
His requirements are structural to the PE role. Get 10 minutes with him before the session starts, not a full 30-minute call. Ask him one question: "What do you need to take back to the portfolio from today?" His answer defines the day-one deliverable.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Every stakeholder has a profile — confirmed or clearly labeled as assumed
- [ ] Fault lines are named specifically — not "they disagree about technology" but what exactly they disagree about
- [ ] The thing nobody will say is identified — even if it's uncomfortable
- [ ] Workshop positioning guidance is specific enough to act on — not generic facilitation advice
- [ ] Confidence levels are honest — assumed insights are labeled, not presented as confirmed
